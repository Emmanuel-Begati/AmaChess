Chapter 3: Mobility Mobility is the key to chess strategy. A piece's mobility is determined by the number of squares it can move to. The more mobile a piece is, the more effective it is. A piece with limited mobility is often a liability. Diagram 3.1: A position illustrating the importance of mobility. (Clickable diagram)

In this position, White's knight on a1 is severely restricted. It can only move to b3 or c2, and neither of these squares is particularly useful. Black's knight on f6, on the other hand, has a wide range of options and can easily influence the game. To improve mobility, consider the following: 1. Centralize your pieces: Pieces in the center of the board generally have more mobility than pieces on the edges. 2. Avoid blocking your own pieces: Be careful not to place your pieces in such a way that they restrict the movement of your other pieces. 3. Control key squares: Controlling key squares, such as the center squares or squares in front of your pawns, can increase the mobility of your pieces. By focusing on mobility, you can improve the effectiveness of your pieces and gain a strategic advantage in the game.
